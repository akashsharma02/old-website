DDF-SAM 2.0: Consistent Distributed Smoothing and Mapping

Abstract

  1. No use of conservative approach to avoid information double counting in DDF SAM 1.0
  1. introduction of anti-factor to subtract information i.e., cancel out neighborhood information from shared summarized maps, to replace information in incremental solvers.
  1. Comparison of three proposed summarization methods

Introduction

  1. Previous work introduced SAM to the Decentralized Data Fusion (DDF), where multiple robots shared a subset of map variables with neighbors in the form of summarized maps. It had two shortcomings:
    1. To avoid information double counting between robots, a strict separation between robot's local map and it's neighborhood map. Each robot maintained two separate incomplete maps of its environment.
    1. Batch summarization used to marginalize non-shared variables performs a factorization of the local system, which is computationally expensive.
  1. DDF-SAM 2.0 introduces:
    1. The augmented local system to replace separate local and neighborhood maps maintained at each robot.
    1. The Anti-factor?? to avoid double counting of information by down-dating estimates
    1. Three summarization techniques applicable to incremental solvers, using exact marginalizations and approximations.

Summarization

Choosing the set of variables to share among robots allows for constrained communication cases. DDF-SAM 1.0 used batch elimination approach. DDF-SAM suggests 3 summarization methods:

  1. Schur Complement reordering: Reorder variables periodically, so that the local map LkrL_k^rLkr​﻿ is at the root of the Bayes tree. This method is computationally as expensive as batch summarization but gives the exact density of the local map p(Lkr)p(L_k^r)p(Lkr​)﻿
  1. Naive Bayes Approximate: Make the conservative assumption that any joint distribution can be factored as follows: p(x,y,z)=p(x)p(y)p(z)p(x, y, z) = p(x)p(y)p(z)p(x,y,z)=p(x)p(y)p(z)﻿. The idea is to construct a summarized map out of the marginals of each saved variable. While computing marginal for individual variables by reordering the variables, can get expensive, by caching some of the intermediate computations in the Bayes tree, the marginals can be computed efficiently. Note that the summarized map will be decidedly sparser than the original exact density.
